Single-Agent LLMs Outperform MAS under Equal Token Budget (Tran & Kiela 2026)

M115arXiv 2026 (cs.CL / cs.MA; no formal venue listed)verified source ↗
When test-time compute is normalized to equal thinking-token budgets, single-agent systems match or beat multiple multi-agent architectures on multi-hop reasoning, implying many reported MAS gains are a compute/context confound.

Background

Multi-agent systems are reported to outperform single agents on complex reasoning, but MAS typically consume far more inference compute than the single-agent baselines they are compared against.

Problem

Reported MAS advantages are frequently confounded by extra test-time computation, so it is unclear whether the architecture itself helps or whether MAS simply spend more tokens.

Motivation / Analysis

A fair, compute-normalized comparison is needed to determine whether multi-agent structure delivers intrinsic benefits beyond additional compute.

Key Insight

By the Data Processing Inequality, distributing reasoning across agents cannot add information; under a fixed reasoning-token budget with good context utilization, a single agent is more information-efficient, and MAS only win when single-agent context use degrades or more compute is spent.

Challenges

Enforcing genuinely equal thinking-token budgets across different architectures and models is hard, and the authors surface artifacts in API-based budget control (notably Gemini 2.5) and in standard benchmarks that inflate apparent MAS advantages.

Method

Controlled experiments compare single-agent systems (SAS) against 5 MAS types (Sequential, Debate, Ensemble, Parallel-roles, Subtask decomposition) under matched reasoning-token budgets, supported by an information-theoretic (DPI) argument.

Experimental Setup

Evaluated on multi-hop reasoning benchmarks FRAMES and MuSiQue across three model families: Qwen3, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama, and Gemini 2.5.

Results

Under equal thinking-token budgets, SAS consistently match or outperform MAS on multi-hop reasoning; MAS become competitive only when single-agent context utilization degrades. The abstract does not report specific per-benchmark score numbers.

Limitations

Scope is multi-hop reasoning QA rather than open-ended agentic tasks; conclusions rest partly on a theoretical idealization (perfect context usage), and budget-control artifacts complicate exact comparisons.

Relation to the Field

A negative/deflationary result challenging the value of MAS orchestration; directly motivates the failure-mode framing of family Z and the compute-fairness concerns behind benchmarks like MultiAgentBench and AgentCollabBench.
