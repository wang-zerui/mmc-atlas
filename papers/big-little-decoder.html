Big Little Decoder (Kim et al. 2023)

M008NeurIPS 2023verified source ↗
A small model decodes autoregressively while a large model is called occasionally to refine only uncertain predictions non-autoregressively, via learned fallback and rollback policies.

Background

Autoregressive generation in Transformer LLMs is slow because tokens are produced sequentially with no parallelization, giving high inference latency.

Problem

Reduce end-to-end generation latency by offloading most decoding to a cheap small model while keeping quality close to the large model.

Motivation / Analysis

Most tokens are easy and can be handled by a small model; the large model only needs to intervene on the few uncertain or incorrect predictions.

Key Insight

A fallback policy that decides when to hand control to the large model, plus a rollback policy that lets the large model correct the small model's mistakes, together capture most of the quality at a fraction of the cost.

Challenges

Tuning fallback and rollback thresholds to balance speed against quality; the framework must work plug-and-play without retraining or architecture changes.

Method

BiLD pairs a small and large model of the same family: the small model decodes autoregressively at low cost; a fallback policy triggers the large model to refine predictions non-autoregressively, and a rollback policy corrects earlier inaccurate small-model tokens.

Experimental Setup

Evaluated on machine translation (IWSLT 2017 De-En, WMT 2014 De-En) and summarization (XSUM, CNN/DailyMail) on an NVIDIA T4 GPU.

Results

Up to 2.12x speedup with minimal generation quality degradation (abstract). The method is lossy: outputs are not guaranteed identical to the large model, though degradation is minimal.

Limitations

Not distribution-preserving; quality depends on threshold tuning and how well the small model matches the large one on the target task.

Relation to the Field

An early strong-weak collaborative decoding scheme related to speculative decoding, but uses learned fallback/rollback control rather than exact rejection sampling, trading strict losslessness for simplicity.
