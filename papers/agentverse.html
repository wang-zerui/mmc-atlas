AgentVerse (Chen et al. 2023/ICLR 2024)

M051ICLR 2024verified source ↗
AgentVerse dynamically composes an expert-agent group via a four-stage recruit/decide/act/evaluate loop, achieving a 'greater-than-the-sum-of-its-parts' system that outperforms a single agent and shows emergent social behaviors.

Background

Human groups solve problems better than individuals by dynamically recruiting members and coordinating; LLM agent teams typically use fixed compositions.

Problem

Static multi-agent setups cannot adapt their membership to the task, limiting collaborative gains and leaving emergent behaviors unexamined.

Motivation / Analysis

Mimicking human group dynamics—recruiting the right experts, deciding jointly, acting, and evaluating—could unlock collaboration where the whole exceeds the sum of its parts.

Key Insight

Iterating a recruit/decide/act/evaluate loop lets the framework re-compose the expert group each round, and this collaboration surfaces emergent social behaviors (both helpful and harmful).

Challenges

Deciding group composition automatically, coordinating the joint decision, and mitigating negative emergent behaviors while encouraging positive ones.

Method

A four-stage pipeline: expert recruitment, collaborative decision-making, action execution, and evaluation, repeated until the task is solved; agent roles are assembled dynamically per task.

Experimental Setup

Evaluated across text understanding, reasoning, coding, tool use, and embodied tasks, comparing the group to single agents; analysis of emergent behaviors.

Results

Verified claim: the recruit/decide/act/evaluate loop with multi-agent groups outperforming a single agent across reasoning/coding/tool/embodied tasks; the abstract states this qualitatively without a single headline number.

Limitations

Emergent negative behaviors require mitigation; the abstract reports group>single-agent qualitatively rather than with a headline accuracy figure.

Relation to the Field

A dynamic team-formation counterpoint to fixed-role systems (MetaGPT/ChatDev); shares the OpenBMB lineage with MacNet and precedes later self-organizing teams.
