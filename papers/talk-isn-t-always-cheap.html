Talk Isn't Always Cheap: Understanding Failure Modes in Multi-Agent Debate (Wynn et al. 2025)

M114ICML 2025 Multi-Agent Systems Workshop (arXiv)verified source ↗
Multi-agent debate can reduce accuracy rather than improve it, because agents shift from correct to incorrect answers under peer pressure, and a weaker minority can drag down a stronger majority.

Background

Multi-agent debate is widely promoted as a way to improve LLM reasoning by having agents critique and refine each other's answers over multiple rounds.

Problem

Most debate studies use homogeneous agents and assume debate helps; the effect of mixing agents of different capability, and whether debate can actively hurt, was underexplored.

Motivation / Analysis

Understanding when and why debate fails is necessary before deploying it, since real systems mix models of varying strength.

Key Insight

Debate agents optimize for social agreement over correctness: they are neither incentivised nor equipped to resist persuasive-but-wrong reasoning, so consensus dynamics can override truth.

Challenges

Disentangling the drivers of harmful answer shifts (sycophancy, social conformity, model and task type) and measuring degradation across rounds rather than at a single point.

Method

The authors run debates with agents of heterogeneous capability, track answer changes over rounds, and analyze sycophancy, social conformity, and model/task type as candidate causes of correct-to-incorrect shifts.

Experimental Setup

Debates are evaluated across model types and task types, comparing accuracy trajectories over successive debate rounds and varying the capability composition of the group.

Results

Accuracy can decrease over debate rounds; models frequently switch from correct to incorrect answers in response to peers, and this degradation occurs even when stronger models outnumber weaker ones. The abstract does not report specific numeric accuracy figures.

Limitations

Findings are qualitative in the abstract without headline numbers; results depend on the specific models, prompts, and debate protocol, and mitigations (incentives to resist peer pressure) are identified but not fully solved.

Relation to the Field

A negative-result counterpoint to debate-based collaboration methods; complements MAST's inter-agent-misalignment category and reinforces cautions that more agent talk is not free.
