The Cost of Consensus: Isolated Self-Correction Prevails Over Unguided Homogeneous Multi-Agent Debate (Bertalanic & Fortuna 2026)

M158ACM Conference on AI and Agentic Systems 2026verified source ↗
A NEGATIVE controlled study: for 7-8B homogeneous agent teams, unguided peer debate costs 2.1-3.4x more tokens for equal or worse accuracy than isolated self-correction, and it identifies three knowledge-destruction pathways.

Background

It is widely assumed that multi-agent LLM debate filters out hallucinations via peer review. This paper tests that assumption for small (7-8B) homogeneous teams without structured roles.

Problem

Does unguided peer exchange among homogeneous LLM agents actually improve the cost-accuracy tradeoff compared to a single agent correcting itself in isolation?

Motivation / Analysis

Debate's overhead is rarely charged against its accuracy gains; a fair matched-cost comparison against isolated self-correction is needed, plus a stochastic control to check whether any benefit is real signal or just perturbation.

Key Insight

For homogeneous small models, peer exchange destroys rather than filters knowledge through three pathways — sycophantic conformity, contextual fragility, and consensus collapse — so isolated self-correction gives a strictly better cost-accuracy tradeoff.

Challenges

Isolating debate effects from mere extra sampling, controlling communication density and temperature, and separating genuine peer information from noise (hence the unrelated-rationale control).

Method

Controlled empirical study with teams of N=10 homogeneous agents over R=3 rounds across three models (Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Ministral-3-8B) on GSM-Hard and MMLU-Hard, comparing peer debate, isolated self-correction, and a stochastic noise control (rationales from unrelated problems); ablates communication density K in {2,4,9} and temperature T in {0.4,0.7}.

Experimental Setup

GSM-Hard and MMLU-Hard, three 7-8B models, debate vs. self-correction vs. noise control, with density and temperature ablations.

Results

NEGATIVE: isolated self-correction consistently offers a better cost-accuracy tradeoff; debate consumes 2.1-3.4x more tokens (up to 28,631 tokens/problem) for equal or lower accuracy. Failure pathways: sycophantic conformity (modal adoption up to 85.5%), contextual fragility (vulnerability rate up to 70.0%), consensus collapse (oracle gap up to 32.3 percentage points). Conformity is already high at minimal peer exposure (K=2) and worsens with more initial diversity.

Limitations

Scoped to the 7-8B homogeneous class without structured roles; the authors do not claim the result extends to larger, heterogeneous, or role-structured teams.

Relation to the Field

An empirical 2026 confirmation and extension of Huang et al.'s matched-budget critique (M059); its conformity/collapse pathways overlap with M162 and M163, and its mechanism is unified under M161's detection-without-correction framing.
