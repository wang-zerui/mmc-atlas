Debating with More Persuasive LLMs Leads to More Truthful Answers (Khan et al. 2024)

M061ICML 2024verified source ↗
In judge-mediated debate for scalable oversight, optimizing debaters to be more persuasive raises a non-expert judge's accuracy, and adversarial debate beats non-adversarial consultancy and naive baselines.

Background

As models surpass human expertise, oversight shifts to 'non-experts overseeing experts'. Debate (Irving et al. 2018) proposed adversarial argument as a mechanism, but empirical evidence with LLMs was limited.

Problem

Can a weaker/non-expert judge (LLM or human) that lacks the underlying information reliably identify the correct answer by watching stronger expert models debate, and does optimizing debaters for persuasiveness help or hurt truth?

Motivation / Analysis

If more persuasive debaters made judges less accurate, debate would be a dangerous oversight tool; the authors test whether persuasiveness and truthfulness align in an information-asymmetric setting.

Key Insight

Under information asymmetry, adversarial structure ties persuasiveness to truth: optimizing experts (higher Elo) for persuasiveness increases the non-expert judge's accuracy, so debate is a viable oversight mechanism even without ground truth.

Challenges

Constructing genuine information asymmetry (experts have evidence, judges do not), optimizing debaters for persuasiveness unsupervised, and comparing LLM vs human judges fairly.

Method

Two LLM 'expert' debaters each argue for a different answer on reading-comprehension-style questions where they have access to the source text and the judge does not; a non-expert judge (LLM or human) picks the winner. Debaters are optimized for persuasiveness in an unsupervised way, compared against consultancy and naive baselines.

Experimental Setup

Information-asymmetric QA with LLM judges and human judges, comparing debate vs. naive baselines and consultancy, and measuring effect of debater Elo/persuasiveness on judge accuracy.

Results

Non-expert LLM judges reach 76% accuracy with debate vs. 48% naive baseline; human judges reach 88% with debate vs. 60% baseline. Higher-Elo (more persuasive) debaters yield more truthful judgments, and debate/interactive-debate beats consultancy; humans still outperform LLM judges.

Limitations

Task is a constructed information-asymmetric QA setting; results may not transfer to domains where persuasiveness and truth diverge, and LLM judges remain below humans.

Relation to the Field

Positive empirical evidence for debate-as-oversight (M060); contrasted with the more cautious weak-judge results of Kenton et al. (M062), and relevant to adversarial-peer risks in M160.
