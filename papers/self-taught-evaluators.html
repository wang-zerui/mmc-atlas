Self-Taught Evaluators (Wang et al. 2024)

M073arXiv preprint (Meta FAIR)verified source ↗
An iterative loop trains an LLM-as-judge on synthetic contrastive outputs with no human preference labels, rivaling reward models trained on human data.

Background

Strong LLM evaluators (judges/reward models) normally require large amounts of expensive human preference annotation that quickly goes stale as models improve.

Problem

Human preference labels are costly and outdated, limiting how fast evaluator models can be built and improved.

Motivation / Analysis

If an evaluator can be bootstrapped from a model's own generations, evaluation can improve continuously without new human labels.

Key Insight

Given an instruction, one can synthesize a contrastive pair (a good response and a deliberately worse one), have the model generate reasoning traces and judgments, keep the correct ones, and iterate to self-improve the judge.

Challenges

Synthetic contrastive pairs must be reliably labelable; the iterative loop risks reinforcing its own errors without a ground-truth anchor.

Method

Starting from unlabeled instructions, the method generates contrasting response pairs, produces judge reasoning traces, trains on the self-generated judgments, and repeats iteratively, using only synthetic data.

Experimental Setup

Evaluated on RewardBench and other judge benchmarks with a Llama-3-70B-Instruct base, compared to reward models trained on human labels and to GPT-4 as judge.

Results

On Llama-3-70B-Instruct it improves RewardBench from 75.4 to 88.3 (88.7 with majority vote) using synthetic data only, beating a GPT-4 judge (confirmed in abstract).

Limitations

Relies on the base model being strong enough to generate and discriminate quality; self-training loops can amplify biases absent human grounding.

Relation to the Field

Complements LLM-as-Judge by removing its human-label dependency; contrasts with panel/ensemble approaches (PoLL, CARE) that reduce single-judge bias instead of retraining the judge.
