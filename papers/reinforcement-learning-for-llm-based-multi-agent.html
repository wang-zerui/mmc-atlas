Reinforcement Learning for LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems through Orchestration Traces (Zhang 2026)

M165arXiv 2026 (survey/position, preprint)verified source ↗
A survey/position paper organizing RL for multi-agent orchestration around 'orchestration traces'—temporal interaction graphs of spawn/delegate/communicate/tool-use/aggregate/stop events—along reward design, credit signals, and five orchestration sub-decisions; it flags that no existing RL method trains the stopping decision.

Background

RL is increasingly used to optimize LLM agents, but mostly for individual actions rather than how a team delegates, communicates, aggregates, and stops.

Problem

There was no unifying framing for how RL should optimize orchestration-level decisions across an agent team.

Motivation / Analysis

A trace-based lens could organize scattered reward/credit/decision techniques and expose gaps between academic RL and industrial swarm deployments.

Key Insight

Representing team behavior as 'orchestration traces' (temporal interaction graphs over spawn, delegation, communication, tool use, return, aggregation, and stopping events) unifies RL design across reward, credit, and decision axes.

Challenges

Assigning sparse counterfactual message-level credit, designing orchestration rewards (parallelism speedup, split correctness, aggregation quality), and the absence of any RL method for the stopping decision.

Method

Surveys the literature along three axes—reward design (eight families incl. orchestration rewards), reward/credit signals (eight units from token to team), and orchestration learning (five sub-decisions: when to spawn, whom to delegate to, how to communicate, how to aggregate, when to stop)—and releases a tagged paper pool, exclusion log, and a JSON schema for replayable orchestration traces.

Experimental Setup

No new experiments; connects academic work to industrial evidence (Kimi Agent Swarm, OpenAI Codex, Anthropic Claude Code) and analyzes a scale gap.

Results

Key verified finding: as of the survey there is no explicit RL training method for the stopping (when-to-stop) decision, and a scale gap exists between reported industrial deployments and open academic evaluation. Confirmed vs abstract.

Limitations

A survey/position paper (arXiv only) with no new empirical results; industrial claims rest on reported deployments rather than verified traces.

Relation to the Field

Provides the RL framing for the orchestration mechanisms studied across this family (AOrchestra, Magentic-One, Anthropic research system) and motivates learning the stopping and delegation decisions.
