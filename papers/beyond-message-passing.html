Beyond Message Passing: A Semantic View of Agent Communication Protocols (Yuan et al. 2026)

M097arXiv 2026 (preprint)verified source ↗
This paper organizes agent communication protocols into a three-layer taxonomy (communication, syntactic, semantic) inspired by Shannon-Weaver and Gricean pragmatics, and shows that current protocols are mature on transport/syntax but under-support semantic alignment such as clarification and repair.

Background

Agent communication protocols are emerging as core infrastructure for LLM systems that use tools, coordinate with other agents, and operate across diverse environments.

Problem

Existing protocol design and comparison focus on transport and message formats, obscuring whether protocols actually help agents reach shared understanding.

Motivation / Analysis

A human-communication-inspired lens is needed to reveal what protocols do and do not support at the level of meaning, not just bytes and schemas.

Key Insight

There is a clear imbalance: because protocols provide little native support for clarification, context alignment, and verification, these semantic responsibilities get pushed into prompts, wrappers, and orchestration logic, creating hidden technical debt.

Challenges

Systematically mapping heterogeneous protocols onto a common layered framework and characterizing the semantic gaps and maintenance costs they impose.

Method

A three-layer framework - communication (reliable transport), syntactic (structured interaction), and semantic (meaning-level coordination) - grounded in Shannon-Weaver and Grice, used to systematically analyze and compare protocols.

Experimental Setup

An analytical study: 18 representative protocols are systematically analyzed and compared across the three layers rather than benchmarked empirically.

Results

No quantitative benchmarks; the verified findings are the 3-layer taxonomy, the analysis of 18 protocols, and the conclusion that semantic-alignment responsibilities are offloaded into prompts/wrappers as technical debt, with a research agenda toward semantically robust ecosystems.

Limitations

The work is conceptual and diagnostic; it identifies gaps and offers selection guidance but does not implement or evaluate new semantic-layer protocol mechanisms.

Relation to the Field

A semantically-focused complement to the Ehtesham et al. protocol survey and the ProtocolBench empirical study; its 'shared understanding' agenda motivates memory/semantics protocols like the Mesh Memory Protocol.
