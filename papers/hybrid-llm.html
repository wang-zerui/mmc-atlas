Hybrid LLM (Ding et al. 2024)

M026ICLR 2024verified source ↗
A router assigns queries to a small (edge) or large (cloud) model based on predicted difficulty and a tunable target quality, cutting large-model calls without quality loss.

Background

Large LLMs give high quality but are expensive and cloud-bound; small models are cheap and edge-deployable but weaker. Hybrid inference combines the two.

Problem

Decide per query whether the small or large model should answer, while letting operators dial the desired quality-vs-cost operating point.

Motivation / Analysis

Prior cascades often fixed a threshold or lacked an explicit, tunable quality target; a difficulty-aware router can adapt to deployment constraints at test time.

Key Insight

Query difficulty can be predicted before answering, and for a substantial fraction of queries the small model is essentially as good as the large one, so those can be safely handled cheaply.

Challenges

Training a difficulty predictor that captures the small-vs-large quality gap and exposing a knob that trades quality for cost without retraining.

Method

A router network predicts per-query difficulty / the small-vs-large quality gap; a threshold (tunable at test time to a desired quality level) decides routing to the small or large model.

Experimental Setup

Trains and evaluates the router on benchmark query sets with small/large model pairs; baselines include fixed routing and random assignment.

Results

Up to 40% fewer large-model calls with no drop in response quality; roughly 20% of queries have a non-negative small-vs-large quality gap (verified in claim_checked).

Limitations

Two-model (small/large) setting rather than many-model; savings depend on the difficulty distribution and quality of the gap predictor.

Relation to the Field

A tunable difficulty-based binary router closely related to RouteLLM and to cost-ascending cascades like FrugalGPT/AutoMix.
