More Capable, Less Cooperative? When LLMs Fail at Zero-Cost Collaboration (Yadav et al. 2026)

M229ICML 2026verified source ↗
Even in a frictionless setting where helping others is costless, LLMs fail to follow cooperative goals, and capability does not predict cooperation: o3 reached only 17% of optimal collective performance versus o3-mini's 50%.

Background

Multi-agent LLM systems depend on cooperation, and it is often assumed that more capable models will coordinate better when incentives are aligned.

Problem

It was unclear whether cooperation failures stem from strategic conflict or from a more basic inability to follow cooperative goals, and whether scaling model capability improves cooperation.

Motivation / Analysis

Isolating cooperation from strategic complexity reveals whether coordination problems will be solved simply by building more capable models.

Key Insight

Capability and cooperation are dissociated: even with negligible personal cost and explicit instructions to help, the more capable model cooperated less, so scaling intelligence alone will not solve coordination and deliberate cooperative design is required.

Challenges

Distinguishing cooperation failures from competence failures required a controlled, frictionless environment and a causal decomposition that automates one side of agent communication.

Method

The authors build a frictionless environment where helping neither benefits nor harms the helper, instruct agents to maximize group revenue, and use a causal decomposition (automating one side of communication) plus reasoning-trace analysis to attribute failures.

Experimental Setup

OpenAI o3 and o3-mini (and other models) are evaluated in the zero-cost collaboration setting, with interventions adding explicit protocols and small sharing incentives.

Results

o3 reached only 17% of optimal collective performance while o3-mini reached 50% despite identical instructions, demonstrating capability does not predict cooperation; explicit protocols doubled performance for low-competence models and small sharing incentives helped models with weak cooperation.

Limitations

The frictionless design deliberately strips strategic complexity, so results may not transfer to competitive or high-stakes settings; findings are tied to the specific models and revenue-maximization task studied.

Relation to the Field

A negative result on the behavioral roots of MAS failure, complementing debate-failure (Talk Isn't Always Cheap) and structural-failure (AgentCollabBench) findings, and reinforcing that architecture and protocols matter more than raw capability.
