DSPy (Khattab et al. 2023)

M103ICLR 2024verified source ↗
DSPy is a programming model that abstracts LM pipelines as text transformation graphs of declarative, parameterized modules, plus a compiler that bootstraps demonstrations/finetuning to optimize any pipeline against a metric instead of hand-tuned prompts.

Background

The community increasingly stacks LMs into pipelines to solve complex tasks, but pipelines are implemented with hard-coded prompt templates discovered by trial and error.

Problem

Hand-crafted prompt strings are brittle, hard to maintain, and do not systematically improve, making pipeline development ad hoc.

Motivation / Analysis

A declarative programming model with an optimizing compiler could replace manual prompt engineering with automatic, metric-driven pipeline optimization.

Key Insight

Express LM pipelines as imperative computational graphs where LMs are invoked through parameterized declarative modules; because modules can learn (by creating and collecting demonstrations), a compiler can optimize compositions of prompting, finetuning, augmentation, and reasoning to maximize a metric.

Challenges

Designing module abstractions general enough for diverse tasks, and building a compiler that bootstraps effective demonstrations within minutes rather than requiring extensive manual tuning.

Method

DSPy modules are parameterized and self-bootstrap demonstrations; a compiler optimizes any DSPy pipeline for a chosen metric, applying prompting, finetuning, and augmentation automatically.

Experimental Setup

Two case studies — math word problems (e.g. GSM8K) and multi-hop retrieval/complex QA (e.g. HotPotQA) — compiled to GPT-3.5, llama2-13b-chat, and small models like 770M T5.

Results

Within minutes of compiling, self-bootstrapped pipelines beat standard few-shot prompting by over 25% (GPT-3.5) and 65% (llama2-13b-chat), and beat expert-created demonstrations by up to 5-46% and 16-40%; small compiled models rival expert prompt chains for proprietary GPT-3.5.

Limitations

Reported gains are relative percentages on two task families; effectiveness depends on the chosen metric and available training examples for bootstrapping.

Relation to the Field

A foundational compiler-based approach to compound-system optimization; complementary to TextGrad's text-gradient engine and a common building block for downstream agentic-workflow and MAS optimization work.
