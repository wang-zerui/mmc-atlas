On Scalable Oversight with Weak LLMs Judging Strong LLMs (Kenton et al. 2024, DeepMind)

M062NeurIPS 2024verified source ↗
A broader test of debate as oversight with weak judges over strong debaters: debate beats single-agent consultancy and helps judge accuracy, but the benefit over direct QA is task-dependent and the persuasiveness effect is smaller than earlier work suggested.

Background

Scalable-oversight protocols aim to let humans (proxied here by weaker LLMs) supervise more capable AI. Prior debate evidence (Khan et al. 2024) used mainly a single extractive-QA task with information asymmetry.

Problem

Across a wider range of tasks and judge-agent capability gaps, does debate actually give weak judges an advantage over consultancy and over simply answering the question themselves?

Motivation / Analysis

Generalizing beyond one task is needed before endorsing debate; the authors probe whether debate's advantage is robust or an artifact of extractive QA with information asymmetry.

Key Insight

Debate reliably beats consultancy and helps somewhat, but its advantage over direct QA depends on whether the task has information asymmetry; the endorsement of debate as oversight is weakly positive rather than decisive.

Challenges

Covering diverse task types (math, coding, logic, multimodal, QA) and judge-agent asymmetries, and separating the effect of information asymmetry from the debate mechanism.

Method

Compares three protocols with LLMs as agents and weaker LLMs as judges: debate (two AIs persuade the judge), consultancy (one AI persuades a questioning judge), and direct question-answering (judge alone). Tests assigned vs. self-chosen sides and varies debater strength, across many judge-agent asymmetries.

Experimental Setup

Extractive QA (with/without information asymmetry), mathematics, coding, logic, and multimodal reasoning, under weak-judge/strong-agent regimes.

Results

Debate beats consultancy across all tasks under random side-assignment; debate beats direct QA in extractive QA with information asymmetry but results are mixed for tasks without it. When agents choose their side, judges are less often fooled by the wrong answer under debate than consultancy. Higher-Elo debaters modestly raise judge accuracy — a smaller effect than in prior work.

Limitations

Uses LLM judges as human proxies; the benefit of debate is small and task-dependent, so the paper stops short of a strong endorsement of debate for oversight.

Relation to the Field

A more skeptical companion to Khan et al. (M061) within the scalable-oversight strand of the debate family, tempering the strength of the persuasiveness-improves-truth result.
