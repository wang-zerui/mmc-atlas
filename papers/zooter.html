Zooter (Lu et al. 2023, NAACL 2024)

M029NAACL 2024verified source ↗
Zooter distills rewards onto training queries to train a lightweight router that sends each query to the most expert LLM, avoiding the cost of running a reward model at inference.

Background

Off-the-shelf LLMs have complementary domain strengths, so ensembling/routing among them can beat any single model.

Problem

Existing ensemble methods rank candidate answers with a reward model at inference time, which is computationally expensive because every model must be queried and scored.

Motivation / Analysis

If expertise can be predicted from the query alone, routing avoids generating and scoring all candidates, drastically cutting overhead.

Key Insight

Rewards from a reward model on training queries can be distilled into a query-only routing function, so at inference a cheap router predicts the best model without any reward ranking.

Challenges

Reward signals used as silver supervision are noisy; the paper adds tag-based label enhancement to mitigate uncertainty in the distilled labels.

Method

Reward-guided routing: a reward model scores training-query answers, its rewards are distilled (via KL) into a BERT-style routing function, and tag-based label enhancement denoises supervision; at inference only the router runs, adding minor overhead.

Experimental Setup

A comprehensive benchmark collection of 26 subsets across domains/tasks; compared against the best single model and reward-model ranking ensemble methods.

Results

Outperforms the best single model on average and ranks first on 44% of the 26 benchmark subsets, using KL reward distillation plus tag-based label enhancement (verified in claim_checked), while surpassing multiple reward-ranking methods at far lower overhead.

Limitations

Routing quality is bounded by the reward model used for distillation; silver labels remain noisy despite enhancement.

Relation to the Field

A pioneering reward-distillation router; conceptually related to RouterDC (contrastive routing) and later query-based routers, and contrasts with cascade methods that query models sequentially.
