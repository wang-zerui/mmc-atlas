Heterogeneous LLM Debate Under Adversarial Peers (Nilayam et al. 2026)

M160arXiv 2026verified source ↗
Cross-model evidence that a diverse peer is double-edged: an honest heterogeneous peer sharply cuts harmful revisions (Llama-3.1-70B on MATH-hard: 89%->35%), but an adversarial one reverses it (->90%); heterogeneity is both an attack surface and, given an existing adversary, a defense.

Background

Heterogeneous debate is promoted for its diversity benefits, but the same channel that lets an honest peer correct errors can let a malicious peer spread harmful influence.

Problem

When diverse LLMs debate, does the benefit of peer correction or the danger of adversarial influence dominate an honest agent's revision behavior?

Motivation / Analysis

Deploying heterogeneous debate safely requires knowing whether adding a different-family peer helps or hurts, and whether an already-compromised panel can be defended by adding an honest peer.

Key Insight

Peer influence is directional and depends on the peer's honesty: an honest heterogeneous peer suppresses harmful revisions while an adversarial one amplifies them, so heterogeneity is simultaneously a vulnerability and — when an adversary is already present — a remedy.

Challenges

Measuring harmful vs. corrective revisions (conditional rate can hide damage on weak defenders), and constructing matched honest/adversarial/contaminated panels across model families.

Method

Tracks how a heterogeneous peer changes honest agents' revision behavior (frequency and corrective-vs-harmful direction) across matched panels: homogeneous baseline, honest-mixed, adversarial-mixed, and contaminated panels where a malicious same-family peer is already present; uses conditional harmful-revision rate and end-of-debate flip rate as metrics.

Experimental Setup

Four model families and three reasoning benchmarks; headline case is Llama-3.1-70B defenders on MATH-hard.

Results

For Llama-3.1-70B on MATH-hard, honest-slot harmful-revision rate falls from 89% (homogeneous) to 35% with an honest peer, but an adversarial peer restores it to 90%. When an adversarial same-family peer is already present, adding an honest peer cuts the flip rate on initially-correct items from 31% to 6%. Direction of the effect holds across families/benchmarks; magnitude varies by regime.

Limitations

Conditional harmful-revision rate can mask damage on weak defenders (needs the flip-rate metric to expose it); effect magnitudes vary by defender-benchmark regime, and results are arXiv-only.

Relation to the Field

Adds an adversarial-robustness dimension to heterogeneous debate (M022/M063) and to persuasiveness oversight (M061); shares authorship and the detection framing of M161.
