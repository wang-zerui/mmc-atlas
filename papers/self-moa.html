Self-MoA / Rethinking Mixture-of-Agents (Li et al. 2025)

M036arXiv 2025verified source ↗
A negative result for mixing: aggregating repeated samples from the single best-performing model (Self-MoA) beats standard Mixture-of-Agents that mixes different LLMs.

Background

Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) aggregates responses from multiple distinct LLMs under the assumption that model diversity is beneficial.

Problem

Is mixing different LLMs actually beneficial, or does it dilute quality by including weaker models?

Motivation / Analysis

MoA performance is highly sensitive to the quality of proposer outputs, so mixing in lower-quality models may hurt more than diversity helps.

Key Insight

Diversity and quality trade off: mixing different LLMs often lowers the average quality of the ensemble, so aggregating many samples from one strong model can be superior.

Challenges

Determining when diversity helps versus hurts, and scaling single-model aggregation without exceeding context limits.

Method

Self-MoA aggregates outputs from only the single best-performing LLM; a sequential variant (Self-MoA-Seq) aggregates outputs incrementally across rounds, as effective as aggregating all at once while relaxing context constraints.

Experimental Setup

Benchmarked against standard mixed MoA on AlpacaEval 2.0, MMLU, CRUX, and MATH, with a systematic study of the diversity-vs-quality trade-off.

Results

Self-MoA beats standard mixed MoA by 6.6% on AlpacaEval 2.0 and 3.8% on average across MMLU/CRUX/MATH, and set a new AlpacaEval 2.0 SOTA when applied to a top model (confirmed in abstract).

Limitations

It also identifies scenarios where mixing distinct models does help, so the single-model prescription is not universal; still bounded by the best model's capability.

Relation to the Field

A direct rebuttal to the diversity premise of MoA (M001); its 'single model with perturbation' theme is pushed further by Self-Consistent MoA (M117), while Co-Failure Ceiling (M135) argues low-correlation heterogeneous ensembles can beat Self-MoA.
