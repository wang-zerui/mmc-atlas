RewardBench 2 (Malik et al. 2026, ICLR'26)

M183ICLR 2026verified source ↗
A harder, multi-skill, accuracy-based reward-model benchmark on newly-sourced human prompts where models score ~20 points lower than RewardBench-1 yet correlate strongly with downstream best-of-N and PPO performance.

Background

RewardBench-1 became the standard reward-model benchmark but is now saturated and may leak into training, weakening its discriminative power.

Problem

Existing reward-model benchmarks are too easy and no longer predict how a reward model will perform when used downstream for alignment.

Motivation / Analysis

A harder, cleanly-sourced benchmark is needed that both challenges current reward models and correlates with real downstream utility.

Key Insight

Building accuracy-based evaluation over multiple skills using newly-collected human prompts produces a much harder benchmark whose scores still track downstream RLHF outcomes.

Challenges

Sourcing fresh, uncontaminated human prompts and designing subsets that measure distinct skills without becoming trivially gameable.

Method

RewardBench 2 comprises 1,865 cases across 6 subsets (Factuality, Focus, Math, Precise Instruction-Following, Safety, Ties), scored by accuracy, and its scores are correlated with downstream best-of-N and RLHF/PPO results.

Experimental Setup

Evaluation of many reward models on the 6 subsets, plus correlation analysis against best-of-N selection and PPO-trained model performance.

Results

Models score roughly 20 points lower than on RewardBench-1, yet RewardBench 2 scores correlate strongly with downstream best-of-N and RLHF/PPO performance; 1,865 cases across 6 subsets (verified vs abstract and journal-ref, ICLR 2026).

Limitations

As a static benchmark it can still be over-optimized over time; correlation with downstream performance is empirical and may vary by training recipe.

Relation to the Field

Successor benchmark to the trained-verifier line (GSM8K/Let's-Verify/Generative Verifiers) and a reward-model analogue to JudgeBench's harder objective evaluation.
