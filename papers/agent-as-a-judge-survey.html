Agent-as-a-Judge Survey (You et al. 2026)

M184arXiv preprint (Jan 2026)verified source ↗
The first comprehensive survey of the shift from LLM-as-Judge to Agent-as-Judge, where agentic judges add planning, tool-augmented verification, multi-agent collaboration, and persistent memory to overcome single-pass judge biases.

Background

LLM-as-Judge has become the default automatic evaluator, but recent work increasingly equips judges with tools, memory, and multi-agent structure.

Problem

Single-pass LLM judges are biased and shallow, and the emerging agentic-judge literature is fragmented without a unifying framework.

Motivation / Analysis

A structured survey is needed to map how agentic capabilities address the known limitations of static LLM judges and to guide future work.

Key Insight

Casting judging as an agentic process, planning, invoking tools to verify claims, collaborating across agents, and retaining memory, directly targets the biases and reliability gaps of single-pass judges.

Challenges

The space lacks a shared taxonomy; agentic judges add cost, complexity, and new failure modes that must be organized and compared.

Method

The survey positions agentic judges (tool-augmented verification, multi-agent collaboration, persistent memory) against LLM-judge limitations and proposes a developmental taxonomy of the field.

Experimental Setup

As a survey it synthesizes and categorizes prior work rather than running new experiments.

Results

Delivers the first comprehensive taxonomy of the LLM-as-Judge to Agent-as-Judge transition (Jan 2026 survey; verified vs abstract); no headline empirical numbers as it is a survey.

Limitations

A survey rather than an empirical study, so it offers organization and direction but no new measurements; an arXiv preprint without a confirmed venue.

Relation to the Field

Umbrella reference for the agentic-judge sub-family in this area, framing AJ-Bench, DataPRM, and AgentV-RL as instances of tool-augmented, environment-aware verification.
