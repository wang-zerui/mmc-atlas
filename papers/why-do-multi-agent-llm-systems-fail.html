Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail? / MAST (Cemri et al. 2025)

M023NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks (arXiv v3)verified source ↗
An empirical study builds MAST, the first Multi-Agent System failure Taxonomy, cataloguing 14 failure modes in 3 categories, derived from human annotation of real MAS execution traces.

Background

Multi-agent LLM systems attract enormous excitement, yet their benchmark gains over single agents are often small and inconsistent, and there was no systematic understanding of why they break.

Problem

Prior work lacked a grounded, empirical account of how and why MAS fail; failures were discussed anecdotally rather than through a structured taxonomy backed by annotated traces.

Motivation / Analysis

A rigorous failure taxonomy plus a labeled dataset would let researchers diagnose MAS problems, target fixes, and measure progress instead of treating failures as opaque.

Key Insight

MAS failures are not just model errors but organizational: they cluster into system-design flaws, inter-agent misalignment, and task-verification breakdowns, so better orchestration and verification matter as much as stronger models.

Challenges

Manually analyzing long multi-agent traces is expensive and subjective; the authors had to establish reliable inter-annotator agreement and then scale annotation without losing fidelity.

Method

Experts analyzed 150 traces to inductively derive the 14-mode / 3-category taxonomy, then an LLM-as-a-Judge pipeline was validated against human labels to scale annotation across MAST-Data (1600+ traces from 7 MAS frameworks).

Experimental Setup

Failure patterns were annotated across 7 popular MAS frameworks and multiple models (GPT-4, Claude 3, Qwen2.5, CodeLlama) on coding, math, and general agent tasks.

Results

14 failure modes in 3 categories; inter-annotator agreement Cohen's kappa = 0.88; task-verification-related failures are a major share (verification failures reported around ~13.48%), and the dataset, taxonomy, and annotator are released publicly.

Limitations

The taxonomy is descriptive rather than prescriptive, offers no guaranteed fixes, and coverage is bounded by the 7 frameworks and models studied; LLM-judge annotation can inherit model biases.

Relation to the Field

Foundational failure-mode reference for family Z; complements benchmark-style diagnoses like AgentCollabBench and attribution work like TraceElephant, and motivates single-vs-multi-agent scrutiny (Tran & Kiela).
