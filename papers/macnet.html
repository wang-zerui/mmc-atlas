MacNet / Scaling LLM Multi-Agent Collaboration (Qian et al. 2024)

M052ICLR 2025verified source ↗
MacNet organizes LLM agents as directed acyclic graphs, scales to 1000+ collaborating agents, and identifies a 'collaborative scaling law' of logistic quality growth, with small-world/irregular topologies performing best.

Background

Neural scaling laws show performance improving with model size; whether adding more collaborating agents helps similarly was open.

Problem

Prior multi-agent work used a handful of agents and did not characterize how collaboration scales with agent count or topology.

Motivation / Analysis

Understanding agent-count scaling and topology effects would guide how to build large collaborative systems efficiently.

Key Insight

Arranging agents on a DAG coordinates reasoning across the topology; overall quality follows a logistic 'collaborative scaling law,' and collaborative emergence appears earlier than neural emergence, with irregular topologies beating regular ones.

Challenges

Coordinating reasoning across many agents without blowup, and finding which topologies best support interactive reflection and refinement.

Method

MacNet (multi-agent collaboration network) places agents on a directed acyclic graph and routes interactive reflection/refinement along edges, supporting collaboration among over a thousand agents.

Experimental Setup

Experiments scaling agent count and varying network topologies, fitting a collaborative scaling law.

Results

Verified claim: supports >1000 agents; irregular/small-world topologies beat regular ones; performance follows a logistic collaborative scaling law with early emergence. Specific per-benchmark magnitudes are in the paper body, not the abstract.

Limitations

Gains saturate under the logistic law (diminishing returns at large N); benefits depend on topology choice and the abstract does not give a single headline accuracy figure.

Relation to the Field

Directly connects role-specialized orchestration to scaling-law analysis; complements later diversity/Ringelmann scaling studies that emphasize heterogeneity over raw agent count.
