Improving Multi-Agent Debate with Sparse Communication Topology (Li et al. 2024)

M063arXiv 2024verified source ↗
Default multi-agent debate is all-to-all; using a sparse communication graph achieves comparable or better accuracy while significantly cutting token/compute cost, on both GPT and Mistral models.

Background

Multi-agent debate improves reasoning/factuality but typically uses a brute-force fully-connected topology where every agent reads every other agent's messages, which is expensive as agents and rounds grow.

Problem

Does every agent really need to communicate with every other agent, or can a sparser topology preserve debate's benefits at lower cost?

Motivation / Analysis

Communication cost in debate scales with the number of connections; if most of the benefit comes from limited peer exposure, pruning connections could dramatically cut cost.

Key Insight

Communication connectivity is a design lever: sparse topologies process far fewer messages yet retain or improve reasoning quality, making the 'society of minds' approach more efficient.

Challenges

Choosing which connections to keep without losing the error-correcting diversity of debate, and validating across models and task modalities.

Method

Systematically studies sparse vs. fully-connected agent communication graphs in multi-agent debate, limiting which agents exchange information each round; evaluates on reasoning plus two new domains (multimodal reasoning and alignment labeling).

Experimental Setup

Reasoning tasks, multimodal reasoning, and alignment labeling, with GPT and Mistral models, comparing sparse vs. fully-connected topologies.

Results

Sparse communication topology achieves comparable or superior performance to fully-connected debate while significantly reducing computational/token cost across the tasks and models. (Abstract states the qualitative result; exact cost-reduction percentages are in the full paper.)

Limitations

Abstract does not give exact numeric accuracy/cost figures; optimal topology may be task- and model-dependent, and gains presume debate helps in the first place (contested by M059/M158).

Relation to the Field

An efficiency-oriented refinement of Du et al.'s multi-agent debate (M002); complements cost critiques (M158) by showing structure, not just budget, governs debate's cost-effectiveness, and relates to topology effects in M163.
