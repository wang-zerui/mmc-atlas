Self-Consistency (Wang et al. 2022)

M017ICLR 2023verified source ↗
Self-consistency samples multiple diverse chain-of-thought reasoning paths from a single model and returns the majority-voted answer, a homogeneous single-model ensemble.

Background

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting elicits step-by-step reasoning, but standard greedy decoding follows only one reasoning path and can commit to an early error.

Problem

Greedy CoT decoding is brittle: a single path may take a wrong turn, and there is no mechanism to recover the correct answer.

Motivation / Analysis

Complex reasoning problems typically admit multiple valid reasoning paths that converge on the same unique correct answer, so agreement across diverse paths signals correctness.

Key Insight

Marginalizing over sampled reasoning paths and taking the most consistent (majority) final answer approximates a self-ensemble without any training or extra models.

Challenges

Requires many samples (higher inference cost), depends on being able to sample diverse-but-valid paths, and applies most naturally to tasks with a well-defined final answer.

Method

Replace greedy decoding: sample a diverse set of reasoning paths via temperature/nucleus sampling, then select the answer that appears most frequently after marginalizing out the reasoning paths.

Experimental Setup

Extensive evaluation on arithmetic and commonsense reasoning benchmarks (GSM8K, SVAMP, AQuA, StrategyQA, ARC-challenge) across large models including PaLM-540B, GPT-3, and UL2.

Results

With PaLM-540B, GSM8K rose 56.5%->74.4% and SVAMP 79.0%->86.6%; reported gains include +17.9% GSM8K, +11.0% SVAMP, +12.2% AQuA, +6.4% StrategyQA, +3.9% ARC-challenge (confirmed in abstract).

Limitations

A homogeneous single-model ensemble that cannot exceed the model's own reasoning distribution; costs scale with sample count and it needs a discrete answer to vote over.

Relation to the Field

The canonical homogeneous ensembling baseline; extended by More Agents (M018), reconsidered by Are More LLM Calls (M040) and Beyond Majority Voting (M132), and used as the tough baseline that multi-agent debate often fails to beat (M042, M043).
