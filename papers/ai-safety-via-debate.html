AI Safety via Debate (Irving, Christiano, Amodei 2018, OpenAI)

M060arXiv 2018 (OpenAI)verified source ↗
A foundational alignment proposal: two agents debate a question and a weaker human judge picks the more truthful/useful side, with the adversarial structure making lying harder than exposing a lie.

Background

A common way to align agents is to have humans judge their behavior, but this fails when the task is too complex for a human to evaluate directly. This predates modern LLMs and frames alignment as an oversight problem.

Problem

How can humans train AI to pursue complex goals correctly when the task exceeds a human's ability to judge the answer directly?

Motivation / Analysis

If a human cannot verify an answer alone, perhaps they can verify it with the help of a competing agent whose incentive is to expose any flaw in the other's argument.

Key Insight

Framed as a zero-sum debate game, truth-telling is favored because it is harder to defend a lie against a capable opponent than to point out that lie; optimal debate lets a bounded judge decide far harder questions than direct judging.

Challenges

Whether real (human or model) judges can be reliably swayed toward truth, and potential failure modes as debaters scale (e.g. persuasive-but-wrong arguments).

Method

Two agents alternate making short statements about a question or proposed action up to a limit, then a human judge decides which agent was most truthful and useful; agents are trained via self-play on this zero-sum game.

Experimental Setup

A proof-of-concept MNIST 'sparse classifier' game where two agents reveal a limited number of pixels to convince a judge classifier, plus theoretical/complexity analysis.

Results

Theoretically, with optimal play debate lets polynomial-time judges answer any question in PSPACE, versus only NP for direct judging. Empirically, the sparse-classifier judge's accuracy rose from 59.4% to 88.9% (6 pixels) and 48.2% to 85.2% (4 pixels) under debate.

Limitations

Only a toy MNIST demonstration; effectiveness on real complex tasks and with real human judges is left as an open empirical question, and robustness under strong persuasive agents is unresolved.

Relation to the Field

The conceptual origin of debate-as-oversight, later empirically tested with LLMs by Khan et al. (M061) and Kenton et al. (M062); its judge-mediated framing distinguishes it from consensus-style debate (M002).
