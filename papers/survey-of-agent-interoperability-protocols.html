Survey of Agent Interoperability Protocols (Ehtesham et al. 2025)

M096arXiv 2025 (preprint)verified source ↗
A survey that compares four emerging agent interoperability protocols - MCP, ACP, A2A, and ANP - along interaction, discovery, communication, and security, and proposes a phased adoption roadmap for building interoperable LLM-agent ecosystems.

Background

LLM-powered autonomous agents must connect to tools, share context, and coordinate across systems, but ad-hoc integrations are hard to scale, secure, and generalize across domains.

Problem

Multiple competing agent-communication standards have emerged with overlapping and unclear scopes, leaving practitioners without guidance on which to use when.

Motivation / Analysis

A structured comparison is needed to clarify each protocol's strengths and deployment niche and to chart how they can be composed into a coherent stack.

Key Insight

The four protocols are complementary rather than competing, and can be layered into a progression from tool access to open decentralized agent networks.

Challenges

Reconciling protocols with different transports, identity models, and discovery mechanisms into a single comparison framework and adoption path.

Method

Systematic comparison of MCP (JSON-RPC tool invocation), ACP (RESTful HTTP with MIME multipart messages and brokered/DID identity, KQML/FIPA-ACL lineage), A2A (capability-based Agent Cards for peer task delegation), and ANP (open-network discovery via W3C DIDs and JSON-LD) across interaction modes, discovery, communication patterns, and security models.

Experimental Setup

A conceptual/analytical survey rather than an empirical study; no benchmark experiments are run.

Results

No quantitative results; the verified contribution is the four-protocol comparison and the layered adoption roadmap: MCP (tools) -> ACP (structured session-aware messaging) -> A2A (collaborative task execution) -> ANP (decentralized agent marketplaces).

Limitations

As a survey it offers framing and guidance rather than empirical measurement of protocol performance or interoperability trade-offs.

Relation to the Field

Provides the taxonomy tying together MCP, A2A, ACP, and ANP; the empirical counterpart is ProtocolBench, and it shares its ecosystem-fragmentation concern with the ICML 2026 'Web of Agents' position paper.
