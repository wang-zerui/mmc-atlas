Multi-Agent Teams Hold Experts Back (Pappu et al. 2026)

M153ICML 2026verified source ↗
A negative result: self-organizing LLM teams consistently fail to match their best individual expert (up to 41.1% loss on ML benchmarks) even when told which agent is the expert; the bottleneck is expert leveraging, not identification.

Background

Multi-agent LLM systems are increasingly deployed as autonomous collaborators that interact freely rather than following fixed workflows, drawing on organizational-psychology notions of team synergy.

Problem

It was unclear whether self-organizing LLM teams achieve human-like synergy—matching or exceeding their strongest member.

Motivation / Analysis

If teams cannot harness their best member, free-form multi-agent collaboration may hurt rather than help on expertise-demanding tasks.

Key Insight

Unlike human teams, LLM teams under-weight expertise via 'integrative compromise'—averaging expert and non-expert views instead of deferring to the expert; the failure is leveraging expertise, not identifying it.

Challenges

Getting agents to properly weight a known expert rather than seek consensus, especially as team size grows.

Method

Studies self-organizing LLM teams on ML benchmarks, including conditions where the expert agent is explicitly identified; performs conversational analysis to diagnose integrative compromise and tests adversarial robustness.

Experimental Setup

Evaluations on ML benchmarks across team sizes, with expert-identification conditions, conversation analysis, and adversarial-agent robustness tests.

Results

Teams lose up to 41.1% versus their best expert on ML benchmarks even when told who the expert is; integrative compromise worsens with team size and negatively correlates with performance, but this consensus-seeking improves robustness to adversarial agents (an alignment vs expertise-utilization trade-off). Confirmed vs abstract.

Limitations

Focused on ML benchmarks; the finding is diagnostic and does not yet provide a fix for the expert-leveraging bottleneck.

Relation to the Field

A cautionary counterpoint to optimistic scaling claims (MacNet); complements Ringelmann-effect and diversity scaling work showing added agents can fail to help.
