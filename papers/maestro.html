Background

LLM multi-agent systems can beat single models but must balance broad exploration of the solution space with convergent synthesis toward a final answer.

Problem

Existing approaches suffer premature consensus, error propagation, and a credit-assignment problem that fails to separate genuine reasoning from superficially plausible arguments.

Motivation / Analysis

Explicitly separating the two cognitive modes — exploring and synthesizing — and giving each a clean learning signal should reduce error propagation and improve credit assignment.

Key Insight

Disentangling the strategic decision (which candidate output to select) from the tactical rationales (why) lets a listwise ranking loss supply strong comparative supervision over justifications while a decision-focused policy gradient handles the selection.

Challenges

Assigning credit correctly across many agents, avoiding rewarding plausible-but-wrong arguments, and combining decision and ranking signals into a stable RL objective.

Method

The Maestro framework runs parallel Execution Agents for diverse exploration and a Central Agent for evaluative synthesis; CLPO combines decision-focused policy gradients with a listwise ranking loss over justifications to train them.

Experimental Setup

Evaluated on mathematical reasoning and general problem-solving benchmarks against state-of-the-art multi-agent methods including Debate, GPTSwarm, AgentPrune, and AFlow.

Results

Delivers absolute accuracy gains of 6% on average and up to 10% at best over state-of-the-art multi-agent methods (Debate/GPTSwarm/AgentPrune/AFlow).

Limitations

Gains are reported on reasoning and general problem-solving benchmarks; the RL objective adds training complexity and the abstract does not detail cost or scaling to many execution agents.

Relation to the Field

An RL credit-assignment method in the same training-optimization family as MAPoRL and Optima, and it directly benchmarks against graph/workflow optimizers GPTSwarm, AgentPrune, and AFlow.
