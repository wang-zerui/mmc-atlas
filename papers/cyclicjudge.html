Background

Evaluating with a full panel of judges on every scenario is the most reliable approach but multiplies cost by the number of judges.

Problem

Running every judge on every item is expensive, yet subsampling naively introduces bias into the estimated mean panel score.

Motivation / Analysis

One wants the accuracy of a full-panel average at the cost of a single judge per item.

Key Insight

Assigning judges to scenarios in a round-robin (balanced) design makes the resulting estimator recover the full-panel mean exactly under a fixed budget, and a variance decomposition shows why this allocation is optimal.

Challenges

Designing the assignment so that judge and scenario effects balance out, and proving optimality under a fixed evaluation budget.

Method

CyclicJudge round-robins judge-to-scenario assignments and analyzes the estimator via a variance decomposition into scenario, generation, judge, and residual components, proving it optimal for a fixed budget.

Experimental Setup

Validation on MT-Bench and MindEval comparing the round-robin estimator's fidelity to the full-panel mean at reduced cost.

Results

The round-robin assignment recovers the full-panel mean at single-judge cost and is shown optimal for a fixed budget, validated on MT-Bench and MindEval (verified vs abstract; no numeric magnitudes reported in the abstract).

Limitations

Recovers the panel mean but not every per-item panel verdict; an arXiv preprint without a confirmed venue, and gains depend on the variance structure holding.

Relation to the Field

A cost-efficiency refinement of PoLL-style juries, complementary to CARE's confounder-aware aggregation, both aiming to get panel-quality evaluation cheaply.
