Z. Benchmarks & Failure modes

9 papers
How these systems are measured — and how they fail.

Benchmarks. The field is building instruments to measure multi-model systems rather than assume their gains. RouterBench (M028/M113) standardizes cost-quality routing evaluation over 405k pre-generated outcomes; MultiAgentBench (M116) scores milestone KPIs across star/chain/tree/graph topologies (graph best in research, ~3% cognitive-planning gain); MASBENCH (M217, ICML'26) spans five axes (Depth/Horizon/Breadth/Parallel/Robustness); AgentCollabBench (M225, 900 tasks) diagnoses process-level failures under a fixed protocol; and failure-attribution suites — TraceElephant (M227, ACL'26) and TELBench+DRIFT (M228) — pinpoint which agent and step caused a failure.

Failure modes. The recurring message is that reported multi-agent gains are often compute or context confounds, not architectural wins. MAST (M023) catalogs 14 failure modes in 3 categories (specification, inter-agent misalignment, task verification) across 1,600+ traces and 7 frameworks (kappa 0.88); single agents match or beat 5 MAS architectures at equal token budget (M115); 'More Capable, Less Cooperative?' (M229, ICML'26) shows capability does not predict cooperation even when helping is free (o3 17% vs o3-mini 50% of optimal); and KV-cache reuse that speeds generation silently breaks LLM judges by weakening cross-candidate attention (M226). Together these define the evaluation discipline the rest of the atlas leans on: compute-matched baselines, and verification of the claimed win.

When test-time compute is normalized to equal thinking-token budgets, single-agent systems match or beat multiple multi-agent architectures on multi-hop reasoning, implying many reported MAS gains are a compute/context confound.

Where Do Deep-Research Agents Go Wrong? / TELBench + DRIFT (2026)
Span-level error localization for long deep-research agent trajectories: TELBench is a 1,000-instance benchmark for spotting harmful error spans, and DRIFT is a claim-centric auditing framework that improves localization by up to 30 percentage points.

More Capable, Less Cooperative? When LLMs Fail at Zero-Cost Collaboration (Yadav et al. 2026)
Even in a frictionless setting where helping others is costless, LLMs fail to follow cooperative goals, and capability does not predict cooperation: o3 reached only 17% of optimal collective performance versus o3-mini's 50%.
