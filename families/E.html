E. Verifier-Gated & LLM-as-Judge

19 papers
One model scores, verifies, or gates another's output.

Base. Trained verifiers gate best-of-N (ORM M071, PRM M070, GenRM M072);
compute-optimal verifier search lets a small model beat a 14× larger one (M078);
LLM-as-judge reaches >80% human agreement (M016) but is position/verbosity-biased
(M076) and near-random on hard objective pairs (M077, JudgeBench); panels of small
disjoint judges beat one big judge more cheaply (M075, PoLL). 2026 SOTA. Two
moves. (i) *Agentic verification*: Agent-as-Judge (survey M184; AJ-Bench M185,
ACL'26), environment-aware generative PRMs (DataPRM M186, KDD'26: +7.21%
ScienceAgentBench), and RL-trained deliberative verifiers (AgentV-RL M187, ACL'26:
4B beats SOTA ORMs by 25.2%) turn the judge into a tool-using agent. (ii) *Reliability
audits*: RewardBench 2 (M183, ICLR'26) is ~20pt harder yet predicts downstream
BoN/PPO; but Reliability-without-Validity (M181, ~541k judgments) shows exact-match
agreement inflates judge quality vs chance-corrected κ by 33–41pp, shifting
rankings up to 14 positions; Judging-the-Judges (M182, TMLR'26) shows a debiased
mid-tier judge beats frontier judges ~15× cheaper (style bias >> position bias);
CARE (M188) and CyclicJudge (M189) fix confounded/expensive panels. Verification
is the most defensible *quality* lever — with the caveat that judge reliability is
itself now a measured, and often overstated, quantity.

Papers in this family

Reliability without Validity (Norman et al. 2026)
The largest LLM-as-judge audit to date shows exact-match agreement inflates apparent judge quality relative to chance-corrected agreement, so a judge can be reliable yet invalid.
M181arXiv preprint (no venue confirmed)
Judging the Judges (Soumik 2026, TMLR)
A systematic comparison of debiasing strategies across judges and provider families finds a debiased mid-tier judge can beat frontier judges at ~15x lower cost, and that style bias dominates position bias.
M182TMLR 2026
RewardBench 2 (Malik et al. 2026, ICLR'26)
A harder, multi-skill, accuracy-based reward-model benchmark on newly-sourced human prompts where models score ~20 points lower than RewardBench-1 yet correlate strongly with downstream best-of-N and PPO performance.
M183ICLR 2026
Agent-as-a-Judge Survey (You et al. 2026)
The first comprehensive survey of the shift from LLM-as-Judge to Agent-as-Judge, where agentic judges add planning, tool-augmented verification, multi-agent collaboration, and persistent memory to overcome single-pass judge biases.
M184arXiv preprint (Jan 2026)
AJ-Bench (Shi et al. 2026, ACL'26 Findings)
An environment-aware benchmark for Agent-as-a-Judge across search, data, and GUI domains that tests a judge's information acquisition, state verification, and process verification through tool and environment interaction.
M185ACL 2026 Findings
DataPRM (Qiu et al. 2026, KDD'26)
An environment-aware generative process reward model that acts as an active verifier interacting with the environment to catch silent errors, using reflection-aware ternary rewards for agentic data analysis.
M186KDD 2026
AgentV-RL / Agentic Verifier (Zhang et al. 2026, ACL'26)
Reframes reward modeling as a multi-turn, tool-augmented deliberative process with forward and backward verification agents, RL-trained so the verifier itself scales at test time under both parallel and sequential test-time scaling.
M187ACL 2026
CARE: Confounder-Aware Aggregation (Zhao et al. 2026)
A judge-ensemble aggregation that models scores as true quality plus shared confounders (verbosity, style), separating signal from bias without ground-truth labels; naive majority/average voting can amplify correlated judge errors.
M188arXiv preprint
CyclicJudge (Zhu et al. 2026)
A round-robin assignment of a judge panel to scenarios that recovers the full-panel mean score exactly at single-judge cost, backed by a variance decomposition over scenario, generation, judge, and residual factors.
M189arXiv preprint
Generative Verifiers / GenRM (Zhang et al. 2024)
Training the verifier with next-token prediction (verify-by-generating with chain-of-thought) beats discriminative reward models and LLM-as-judge on best-of-N.
M072ICLR 2025
JudgeBench (Tan et al. 2024)
On objectively-checkable but challenging response pairs, even strong LLM judges perform only slightly above random, exposing reliability limits.
M077ICLR 2025
Scaling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally (Snell et al. 2024)
Compute-optimal PRM-verifier search plus sequential revisions lets a small model beat a 14x larger one under matched FLOPs, gating quality via a verifier rather than parameters.
M078arXiv preprint (UC Berkeley / Google DeepMind); ICLR 2025
Let's Verify Step by Step / PRM800K (Lightman et al. 2023)
A process reward model scoring each reasoning step is a stronger verifier for best-of-N selection than an outcome reward model.
M070arXiv preprint (OpenAI); ICLR 2024
Self-Taught Evaluators (Wang et al. 2024)
An iterative loop trains an LLM-as-judge on synthetic contrastive outputs with no human preference labels, rivaling reward models trained on human data.
M073arXiv preprint (Meta FAIR)
CriticGPT / LLM Critics Help Catch LLM Bugs (McAleese et al., OpenAI 2024)
An RLHF-trained critic writes natural-language critiques of code that trainers prefer over human critiques and that find more bugs, with human+critic teams hallucinating less than the critic alone.
M074arXiv preprint (OpenAI)
Replacing Judges with Juries / Panel of LLM evaluators (PoLL) (Verga et al. 2024)
A panel of several smaller judges from disjoint model families correlates better with humans, is far cheaper, and has less intra-model bias than one large judge.
M075arXiv preprint (Cohere)
Large Language Models are not Fair Evaluators (Wang et al. 2023)
LLM-as-judge verdicts are strongly position-biased and can be flipped by reordering candidates; multiple-evidence and balanced-position calibration mitigate it.
M076ACL 2024
LLM-as-a-Judge / MT-Bench & Chatbot Arena (Zheng et al. 2023)
Strong LLM judges approximate human preference at scale and exhibit position, verbosity, and self-enhancement biases.
M016NeurIPS 2023 Datasets & Benchmarks Track
Training Verifiers to Solve Math Word Problems (Cobbe et al. 2021, GSM8K)
Best-of-N selection by a trained verifier (outcome reward model) beats plain finetuning and scales better with data than the generator.
M071arXiv preprint (OpenAI)
